Getting started

Three steps to begin

You do not need new resources or extra time. These three steps integrate spaced practice into what you already do.

01
Map your retrieval points
At the start of term, choose four weeks for a 10-minute retrieval task on previous content. Mark them now — weeks 1, 4, 8 and 12 are a strong default for a 13-week term.
02
Design tasks that require recall
Students must produce answers from memory — not recognise them. Paper quizzes, oral questioning, or think-pair-share all work without computers or internet access.
03
Record scores and adjust
Note class average scores at each retrieval point. If scores at week 8 are not higher than week 4, adjust your spacing intervals or task design for the next term.
Research basis

Built on three bodies of evidence

The framework synthesises decades of cognitive science research into principles a classroom teacher can apply directly.

Spacing effect
Distributing practice across time produces stronger long-term retention than massed practice. The optimal gap between sessions scales with how long you want the learning to last (Cepeda et al., 2006).
Cognitive load theory
Spacing reduces cognitive load over time as students build schemas incrementally, freeing working memory for deeper understanding of new content (Sweller, van Merriënboer & Paas, 1998).
Retrieval practice
Active recall — not re-reading or re-watching — is the mechanism that makes spacing work. Effortful retrieval strengthens memory traces far more than passive review (Roediger & Butler, 2011).
